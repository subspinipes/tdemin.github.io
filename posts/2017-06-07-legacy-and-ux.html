How Legacy Compatibility Kills User Experience in Decentralized Environments

June 7, 2017

Timur Demin

Preserving compatibility with legacy products has always been a PITA. The developers who preserve compatibility with other products sometimes notice they cannot implement feature X because it breaks Y for some users.

It is less problematic for those who provide a centralized service with some client software to use the service with. Sometimes such services even deny alternate clients or software because it would break things for other users (e.g. LibreSignal, a deblobbed variant of Signal private messenger, was stopped due to Moxie’s disagreement of LibreSignal’s usage of Signal servers). Moxie Marlinspike, head of Open Whisper Systems, the Signal developers, has even written an article stating his opinion on user experience in decentralized environments or the environments where alternative clients are enabled.

Indeed, cannibalizing a federated application-layer protocol into a centralized service is almost a sure recipe for a successful consumer product today. It’s what Slack did with IRC, what Facebook did with email, and what WhatsApp has done with XMPP. In each case, the federated service is stuck in time, while the centralized service is able to iterate into the modern world and beyond.

Daniel Gultsch, the head developer of another instant messaging client, Conversations, has replied to Moxie’s post stating the biggest problem of federated networks and their user experience is not them being stuck in time, but the lack of developers able to maintain user-friendly IM solutions.

Instead the most frequent reason new features don’t get implement is a lack of resources. The majority of end-user-facing XMPP implementations are developed by people in their spare time.

The lack of manpower is surely a problem for the federated systems as they do not always receive as much attention as they really need. But, well, is the lack of manpower really the biggest problem of the federated solutions?

A look at XMPP

XMPP, the protocol mentioned in both articles, is complete with its extensions, so-called XEPs. The number of extensions present nowadays is pretty overwhelming: there are ≈ 400 extensions reviewed by XSF. Those extent the protocol with features you see in modern messengers: sending files, retrieving message archive, etc.

An ideal server/client should probably support most extensions that are up-to-date and which are useful. But the number of XMPP extensions is really big, so the most urgent ones are implemented first.

Those protocol extensions are written at the time they are needed. An average extension which was a big technological advance yesterday could become obsolete in a few years, so as it became with a bunch of current XMPP extensions like XEP-0136.

XMPP users somehow adopted the way being stuck in time, and most clients that are present include the archaisms like chat tabs (even the most up-to-date mobile XMPP client, Conversations, still uses that tabs concept), while modern messengers like WhatsApp and Telegram just show the chats list, none of which is hidden until the message history is expunged. Some of the actively used clients still don’t even support message syncing between online clients (i.e. Tkabber), not to mention “advanced” extensions like session resumption or message corrections. However, those clients are still used and are even recommended by some.

Those archaisms are actively supported by the users (this GitHub thread would probably explain things better than me: a legacy server user is protecting his beloved XEP-0136, which is already dropped by the two major XMPP server software bundles: Prosody and ejabberd due to its difficulty to maintain). And these archaisms are stopping not only the clients to develop, but the modern way of user experience, which is not adopted due to the old users’ demands.

I think we have much more important tasks than implementing experimental XEPs.

Getting not enough manpower isn’t the only big XMPP problem. Another of its big problems is its users, who are freezing the clients development because of their beloved old client/server not supporting new features.

Another glance at Riot

Riot, an instant messaging client built on top of Matrix, which is promoted by many as an alternative to Slack and XMPP both, is slowly starting to face the same problem from the rear side.

One of the best things about Riot is its idea to be a shared workplace for the web. Riot integrates with XMPP, Slack and IRC by using special bridges attached to the channels. The drawback is, those bridges are meant just to connect people from various networks, so they don’t provide new Matrix features. Those bridges not only render advanced Riot features like multi-device end-to-end encryption unusable, they break things for other participants: no one can even say an IRC bridge user is online or not, and I didn’t even mention things like read receipts et al.

XKCD #1782

There are still lots of people who use IRC, so some Matrix/Riot rooms are mostly accessed by IRC bridges users. However, you need only one IRC user in the room to break advanced Riot features for other users and render chats the same way it was in XMPP, just in a glossy new client.

Riot is another example where the decentralization and freedom of choice just render awesome features unusable due to them being inaccessible to alternate clients users.

This is not the decentralization nature of federated networks who buries them. This is not the lack of manpower who buries the protocols and the networks. Those who are responsible for considering their protocol dead are the users themselves.

No one is responsible for making your instant messaging solution dead, but it’s just you. Think of it when you try to support that old legacy client built in 2006 just because you know many guys who still use it.
